Section 01 — Initial Stance / Personal Position

No AI used in this section.

The power of architecture
is opinion and position

The power of architecture is opinion and position. LLMs are informed by the data of people that can access it, those with sufficient privilege, and best learn their input language. It cannot have its own opinion as it reflects its input. Criticality, restraint and discretion allow the architect to navigate polarities into selected arguments that apply to specific contexts. We also carry situated knowledge, lived experience, and spatial memory, all of which cannot be replicated.

We assume AI can replace jobs that we don't understand, and it is in the gap of our ignorance that AI has begun to thrive. Considering the cross-industry appropriation of "architect", architecture is framed as a way of constructing systems and ordering the world. Architects have the ability to manipulate and represent information graphically and efficiently. If information is power, the architect must become a systems thinker.

My interest in personal knowledge management is informed by ownership, agency, efficiency and self-understanding. To govern how our information is accessed, managed and owned is an act of political resistance. Customisation and empowerment should be prioritised when selecting software, because monopolies and data systems shape how decisions compromise ethical positions.

Environmental freedom is questioned in South Africa, where our constitution contradicts our service delivery. Sustainability in the environment is a default, with economic disparity being a greater concern. Thus, architects hold the skills to navigate societal rebuild, metaphorically and physically. Algorithmic control also produces a loss of identity and community: siloed information acquisition is designed to be divisive, and we no longer know which interests are dictated to us instead of intuitively understood.

The "mistakes" of the hand are lost when architects design fully in software instead of sketching naturally. I have aphantasia, the inability to visualise. I design through bodily intelligence, using kinaesthetic sensory understanding of how spaces feel. An imperfect emotional memory is more powerful than an objective intellectual one, and an AI's attempt at understanding human instinct is lost in translation.

What started with Louis Sullivan's "form follows function" evolved to "form follows finance" and "form follows flow", but I personally believe form follows feeling.

Form-finding has to be eclectic, specific and contextualised. Instead of assuming objectivity, we should assume positionality. A contrarian who always rejects the norm is a conformist of a different kind; no one is ever truly central.

Section 02 — AI Conversation, Reflected and Integrated

AI as mirror —
failing productively

As an autistic-ADHD individual, I conceive architecture through an eclectic process, as I attempt to consolidate, order and organise my own thoughts. AI is a powerful thought mirror in this sense, as it never generates what I want. Through its failures, I am forced to confront the flaws in my communication, and clarify intentions towards a better prompt. I find it now far more effective to prompt incompletely, and allow the dialogue to inspire me to find my own conclusions faster with examples of what I do not want.

I have come to disagree with the principles of prompt engineering when it comes to brainstorming and creative tasks. Even with the perfect prompt, following all best practices, AI still fails to generate responses that capture the character of my concepts. It produces a benchmark of global sameness, a collective bias that does not represent me. I am forced to reflect upon what I would represent differently, and it is in this difference that form is found.

After a long process, detailed in my references and process map, the AI conversation helped me identify five linked ideas in my thinking:

01 Beauty as a primary value
02 Form follows feeling, held in tension with form follows function
03 Architecture as contradiction rather than resolution
04 Radical empathy as both political position and design directive
05 Kinesthetic sensory intuition as a form of intelligence. The body already knows.

The conversation did not give me a new position. It helped me notice the structure of one I already held. Design begins with the question of how a sequence of atmospheres might hold the full, contradictory range of human experience without prescribing how anyone should feel within it. The architect holds binaries open: beauty and function, emotion and intelligence, tradition and disruption, the mundane and the extraordinary. In that tension, meaningful space becomes possible.

Section 03 — Developed Argument and Case Studies

Architecture does not begin
with a diagram

Architecture does not begin with a diagram. It begins with a body moving through space, registering temperature, texture, threshold, and the quality of light before any intellectual rationalisation occurs. The position defended here holds that beauty is not decorative but primary: the first point of somatic attraction from which structural integrity and spatial intelligence are subsequently judged. This is not a romantic claim. It is an epistemological one. The case studies examined below are selected because they produced an immediate, visceral architectural response and because each shows form-finding as a negotiation with what already exists: landscape, earth, ruin, geology, care, and technical process.

Teshima Art Museum

Ryue Nishizawa & Rei Naito — Teshima, Japan

The Teshima Art Museum offers the clearest illustration of atmosphere as sequence rather than prescription. A seamless concrete shell, poured over an earthen mound and excavated from beneath, produces a column-free interior where water springs from the floor and wind enters through two elliptical ceiling openings.

Nothing instructs the visitor how to feel. Instead, ground, light, sound and moving water register on the body and allow emotion to emerge without being directed. The building does not perform meaning; it creates the conditions for it.

That this effect is produced through extreme structural precision — including a twenty-five-centimetre shell and massive earthen formwork — is important. Ethereal spatial experience and rigorous technical process are not opposites. They depend on each other.

House and Restaurant

Junya Ishigami and Associates — Japan

House and Restaurant pursues a related logic through an inverted method. Rather than pouring a shell over earth and removing the mound, Ishigami pours concrete directly into deep soil excavations, using the ground itself as formwork.

The result is a cave-like domestic and commercial refuge where the texture of the earth becomes the texture of the interior: raw, heavy, geological. Where Teshima is open and luminous, this building is subterranean and dense.

Yet both treat form-finding as an encounter with existing physical conditions rather than the imposition of a preconceived image. In both cases, beauty emerges from the specificity of the process rather than from a prior aesthetic decision.

PC Caritas

Architecten de Vylder Vinck Taillieu — Melle, Belgium

PC Caritas extends the argument into the domain of care. Working within the shell of a partially demolished nineteenth-century psychiatric villa in Melle, Belgium, the architects inserted modern glass greenhouses and repaired broken masonry with concrete blocks on site, without predetermined blueprints.

The building holds contradictions that most institutional architecture resolves too quickly: decay and care, openness and enclosure, ruin and repair. Psychological safety does not come from softness or clinical neutrality. It comes from an environment that acknowledges complexity without collapsing it.

The limitations are real, including questions of thermal comfort and regulatory compliance, but these tensions are part of what makes the project instructive rather than ideal.

Ca'n Terra

Ensamble Studio — Menorca, Spain

Ca'n Terra completes the argument by demonstrating what technology's role in this kind of practice should be. An abandoned stone quarry in Menorca is converted into a home through minimal intervention: advanced 3D laser scanning records the geological interior, and precise cuts are made to admit light and air.

The technology does not generate the form. It reveals what is already there. This is the role computation should play in a practice grounded in sensory intuition: not to impose geometric complexity, but to structure and protect the irregularities that already exist in physical matter.

The cave, the geological mass, the found condition carry psychological weight before anyone theorises them.

Form follows function remains a valid demand. But in each case, the functional resolution is achieved through means that simultaneously produce atmosphere, dignity, and sensory specificity.

Across all four projects, a shared formal logic emerges. None begins with an image. Each is a response to what already exists. The architect does not impose; she negotiates. Beauty emerges not as a preconceived aesthetic but as the register of a genuine encounter between building and site, body and material, function and feeling.

This also reframes computational design. Its value is not that it replaces intuition, but that it can measure, test, reveal, or protect conditions too subtle or complex to hold through intuition alone. The buildings feel like they just happened, but that effortlessness is produced through layers of detail, rigour and design.

Section 04 — Conclusion / Reflection

Three key insights

1

I initially interpreted my difficulty with prompt engineering as personal inadequacy. However, the most valuable conversations emerged when I spoke naturally, and misunderstandings exposed ambiguities in my thinking. This forced me to clarify what I actually believed.

2

The case studies also shifted a binary I had carried for a long time. I thought embodied spatial design, hand sketching and analogue feeling were at odds with technological advancement and computational form-finding. Yet the buildings I admire most are highly technical. They feel effortless, like they just "happened," to the landscape — but conceal layers of detail, rigour and design.

3

This process made me recognise that my personal interest in systems design and knowledge management overlaps with computational design in architecture: not just for this essay; but an exciting potential career path.

Section 05 — Revised Personal Position

No AI used in this section.

Not changed —
transformed

My position has not changed. Instead, its intrarelationships have transformed. I still believe that sensory, embodied and intuitive knowledge is just as legitimate as intellectual and analytical knowledge.

However, I now hold these ideas as a deeply intertwined mesh, instead of isolated beliefs; with a stronger appreciation for contradiction. The architecture I find most exciting almost walks a similar contrarian path of balancing tensions between perceived binaries: beauty vs function. Intuition vs analysis. Technology vs craft.

The idea of criticality has extended beyond what I thought I knew — computational tools can be chosen to support care, ownership, and situated responses. Systems thinking might not be at the foundation of my position, but it no longer feels like an isolated personal interest.

We are always in the middle of a movement, repeating revisited strands of our history. We will often be misunderstood. However, we must continue our attempt at articulating our collective longing.

I continue to believe that the manifestation of my architectural values do not require permission or institutional validation. Perhaps, as I am designing, I put something there just because I fucking felt like it (and this has meaning). However, there also needs to be a certain detachment from form-finding philosophy. Ultimately, does my position matter? The building will become whatever its people need it to be.
