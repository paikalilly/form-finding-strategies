Top 5 Core Themes — the Thesis Spine

  1. Beauty as the primary value and the primary argument. Years of position-building have been an attempt to justify the right to call something beautiful without needing institutional permission. This is the spine. Everything else — the phenomenology, the neurodivergence, the decolonial politics — serves this.
  2. Form follows feeling, held in tension with form follows function. Not a rejection of function but a refusal to let function collapse into technical rationalism. The form-finding act is the sequencing of atmospheres that hold multiple lived experiences simultaneously — not a prescribed emotion but a range of conditions.
  3. Architecture of contradiction: holding binaries rather than resolving them. The most spontaneous and emotionally confirmed idea in the transcript. Meaningful design sits within the tension of binaries. This is not a theory — it is a disposition that should govern the entire document.
  4. Radical empathy as both political position and design directive. Not softness — a refusal to let ideology override the human in front of you. In the act of radical empathy, a radical political statement is being made. This connects decolonial politics, neurodivergent design, care architecture, and the critique of spectacle into a single ethical engine.
  5. Kinesthetic / sensory intuition as a form of intelligence. The body already knows. This is not a workaround for aphantasia — it is the faculty itself. Architectural education has spent years demanding justification for something that is already valid. The position is the refusal to keep defending it.

What the Actual Architectural Position Appears to Be — Based Only on These Two Documents

Architecture is a long argument for the right to call something beautiful without having to justify it to a tradition that was never justified in the first place. The position emerges from a body — sensory, neurodivergent, kinesthetically intelligent — that understands space before it can theorise it. Design begins not with shape or logic but with the question of how a sequence of atmospheres might hold the full, contradictory range of human experience without prescribing how anyone should feel within it. The architect does not resolve binaries. She holds them open. Beauty and function. Emotion and intelligence. Tradition and disruption. The mundane and the extraordinary. In that tension — not resolved, not collapsed, but held — meaningful space becomes possible. This is also a political position: refusing to imitate a canon that demanded no justification of itself, and refusing to dehumanise anyone whose politics conflict with that refusal. The act of designing for the person in front of you, in all their sensory specificity and cognitive reality, is already radical. The position does not need a framework to be valid. It needs to be stated clearly, in its own language, once.
