# Gemini Deep Research Prompt

You are helping me prepare the **"Developed Argument & Case Studies"** section of an architecture assignment. Do not write the final essay section for me. Instead, produce research that I can use to write it myself.

## Assignment Context

The required section is a **600-800 word developed architectural argument**. It must:

- expand my architectural position using insights from an AI dialogue process
- include at least two case studies that illustrate form-finding strategies
- refer to relevant theories, authors, and contemporary architectural trends
- suggest useful visuals, such as site photos, drawings, diagrams, models, plans, sections, or atmospheric images

The assignment will later end with a **100-200 word conclusion** reflecting on whether AI confirmed or shifted my thinking, so your research should also help me identify where my position was reinforced, complicated, or redirected.

## My Architectural Position

Summarise this briefly in your response, but do not over-explain it.

My position is that **beauty is a legitimate core value in architecture**, not a decorative afterthought and not something that must be rescued by logic before it matters. I am interested in beauty as the first attraction, with process and integrity creating deeper respect afterward.

I understand architecture through the body as much as through the eye. Space is never neutral: light, sound, texture, threshold, proportion, exposure, refuge, clutter, density, and atmosphere affect people before they can rationally explain why. My position values **embodied spatial intelligence**, sensory experience, and the idea that the body already knows.

I am drawn to architecture that holds contradiction rather than resolving it too neatly: beauty and function, emotion and intelligence, simplicity and complexity, the measurable and immeasurable, the mundane and extraordinary. I am interested in **"form follows feeling"**, but not as a rejection of function. Instead, feeling should be understood as part of how function is lived and perceived.

I think of design as the **sequencing of atmospheres** rather than the production of a single mood or image. Architecture should create material conditions through which different users can assign meaning through perception, memory, movement, and use.

My position is also grounded in **radical empathy**. Architecture should respond to real, complex human beings rather than abstract users. Care should be spatial, material, institutional, and atmospheric: designed through arrival, waiting, retreat, visibility, privacy, acoustics, circulation, refuge, and transition.

Technology and AI are useful when they clarify intention, reveal hidden relationships, or help structure intuition, but they should not replace embodied human judgement. Computation is relevant as a possible way of making invisible relations visible, but it is not yet my established method.

Condensed position: **architecture should turn chaos into beautiful, liveable structure without flattening the human beings who inhabit it. It should hold complexity without becoming generic, create clarity without becoming reductive, and make form as a response to lived emotional, social, and spatial conditions.**

## Research Direction

I like **Junya Ishigami's** architectural aesthetic and philosophy. Use him only as a **calibration reference**, not as the main subject of the research.

Briefly explain why Ishigami aligns with my position, especially in relation to:

- lightness, atmosphere, ambiguity, and fragile spatial conditions
- form that feels intuitive, dreamlike, or almost immaterial
- architecture that blurs object, landscape, body, and perception
- beauty that is not merely decorative

Then move beyond Ishigami. Use him as a starting point to find **less obvious case studies**.

## What To Research

Find architects, practices, buildings, installations, spatial works, or projects that strongly align with my position but are **not the usual obvious starchitect examples**. Prioritise nuanced and less commonly cited case studies over generic examples.

Look especially for case studies involving:

- form-finding through atmosphere, lightness, ambiguity, or sensory perception
- spatial sequences that produce emotional or bodily effects
- architecture that holds contradiction rather than resolving it
- beauty with process, restraint, strangeness, or integrity
- care, psychological safety, refuge, transition, or embodied experience
- material, structural, environmental, or computational strategies that support feeling rather than spectacle
- projects where form emerges from delicate relationships between body, site, climate, memory, landscape, or social conditions

Do not choose the final case studies for me. Give me options and explain their strengths.
