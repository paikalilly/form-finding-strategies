The Peak Shift Principal

How human survival shapes our idea of beauty

“What you want, or what you need,” is what your mother always told you.

But what Momma didn’t know, was that sometimes: it means the same thing.

The Peak Shift principal, established by V.S. Ramachandran, states that it is embedded in our nature to perceive certain things as attractive, and exaggerations of it even more so.

We are stimulated by great art, yet we don’t know why. This impact is supposedly instinctual, and our instincts are developed based on our environment, society, and other living conditions.

The Venus of Willendorf is the one of the oldest perceptions of ourselves: an image of the body. However, she is far from accurate, arms almost vanished, monstrously sized breasts, bulbous rolls of fat, really, that is too out-of-proportion to be healthy. But this is what the ancients wanted, and more importantly, needed: insulation, fertility, rich in plumpness, and their instinct to survive is what they were stimulated by.

and developed impact with their Art

This “special little lady” is a key point in the world of Art. Today, she is treated like a queen, with utmost protection. Yet to any of us, we would wonder why she is so special, why she is appealing, and this is because our idea of beauty has changed with our era. In the severe prehistoric environment of Austria, 39000-28000 BC, our skinny models and their flat tummies would never survive.

But there is hope for our thin arms in the idea of civilisation, one of the first, in fact. The Egyptians rose in the Saharan desert to create a powerful, stable, system of order along the banks of River Nile. One can find numerous Egyptian figurines with the exact same proportion in clear divisions: head, torso, arms, legs. I could be specific, and choose one, like Bast, the cat goddess, or Ra, the sun god, but I really don’t need to. Gazing at the vast inner walls of the Pyramids, for instance, one will find, in utter astonishment, thousands of images of people on a single wall. And they’d look exactly the same, save for hair, clothing and make-up. One could wonder how each leg would measure the same in millimeters, but we uncovered that their artisans used grids to form their images, and anything else, is an imperfection. That’s what they wanted, and, being a great example of an organized society, it’s what they needed: Uniformity.

While this admirable, is such an extreme extent, in our liberal society, that kind of discipline is quite frankly, bland.

You may also wonder how all this is applicable to us in the 20th, 21st century.

We’re all too familiar with Surrealism and abstract art. Just the odd, circus-y, deformed, distorted, freaky, weird and wonderful. Like Mad Men’s season seven cover. All of this came about in the 20th century, starting in the 1920’s: global interaction - trade, transport, and the war, and later, the second war. Our ultimate survival skills were tested, and something that looks like from a dream was the product. We could say that people used art to fight, and to escape the reality of the cruel world. This escape into our dreams was what we needed, for our sanity. How ironic that the savior of our sanity appears as insanity.

But that’s what stimulates us, even to this day, Pop Art sensations sweep the globe, like Lady Gaga, and today, in a society of prejudice, genius, and expectation, we fight for our individuality. So what great Art can you make?
